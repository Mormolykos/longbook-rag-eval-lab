# Diagnosing Hierarchical Retrieval Failure in Long-Document RAG: A LongBook Verifier Ablation Study

This report presents Experiment C, a follow-up ablation study for the LongBook Verifier benchmark. The study diagnoses why the original hierarchical book RAG method underperformed the chapter-summary chain method in Experiment B, a 5-book, 240,767-word long-form narrative corpus with 80 gold questions and five retrieval methods. In the original Experiment B comparison, chapter-summary chain was the strongest original retrieval method by context recall, while hierarchical book RAG came third, not second.

Experiment C compares five variants: the original hierarchical pipeline (`hier_current`), hierarchical retrieval without neighbor expansion (`hier_no_neighbors`), the chapter-summary chain baseline, an oracle-chapter diagnostic variant (`hier_oracle_chapter`), and an oracle-chapter variant with neighbor expansion (`hier_oracle_chapter_neighbors`). The results show that `hier_current` achieved context recall 0.3365 and precision 0.3475, while removing neighbor expansion raised recall to 0.4771 and precision to 0.4000, matching chapter-summary chain. Oracle chapter routing raised recall to 0.7844 and precision to 0.6796.

These results support the original error-compounding hypothesis but show that it was incomplete. First-stage chapter-selection limits can damage downstream retrieval, but neighbor-expansion dilution was also a major failure mode. The study does not establish a universal rule against hierarchical retrieval; it shows that multi-stage retrieval pipelines require stage-level ablation before their failures can be interpreted.
